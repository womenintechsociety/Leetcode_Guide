01
Where You're Starting
Know Your
Starting Point
This guide works for everyone. Pick the path that fits you right now.
👋
You are a complete beginner if...
You know variables, loops, and functions in at least one language — but you've never looked at a LeetCode problem. That's completely fine. This guide starts at zero.

Your actual first steps (before solving a single LeetCode problem):

1.
Get comfortable with arrays & strings
Know how to loop through them, access elements, slice, reverse. This is your bread and butter for the first 50 problems.
2.
Understand HashMaps / Dictionaries
One data structure that unlocks ~30% of easy problems. Practice storing and looking up key-value pairs in your language.
3.
Create your LeetCode account
Go to leetcode.com → Sign up → Head to Explore → Start the "Beginner's Guide" card. No local setup needed — use the browser editor.
4.
Pick Python if unsure
Python's clean syntax means you spend time thinking about the problem, not fighting the language. Easiest for beginners, still widely used in interviews.
💡
Society Tip
Come to the society sessions. Watching someone else think through a problem out loud is one of the fastest ways to learn. You don't need to share your solution — just being there helps.
⚡
You know a bit if...
You've seen some data structures, maybe solved 5–10 easy LeetCode problems, but you don't have a system and you're not consistent. You feel like you "should be further along."

What's likely holding you back:

Solving randomly
Picking problems with no plan. Fix: follow Blind 75 or Striver's SDE Sheet in order.
Looking too fast
Checking solutions after 5 minutes builds an illusion of knowledge, not actual skill.
Inconsistency
Solving 15 problems on Saturday, then nothing for 10 days, then guilt-solving again. Break the cycle.
👥
You're not behind
Everyone in the society is at a different stage. The only comparison that matters is you vs. you from last week.
🔁
You're restarting if...
You burned out before. Maybe you went hard for a week, then stopped. Or you felt like you'd "lost" all your progress. You haven't. Restarting is a skill.

The restart protocol — three things only:

1.
Do one easy problem. Right now.
Not tomorrow. Not after you re-read theory. One problem. Even if it takes 45 minutes. Momentum beats motivation every time.
2.
Set a floor, not a ceiling
Your minimum is 1 problem/day. Not 5. Not 3. One. You can always do more, but 1 is the non-negotiable. This is how you build a streak that survives exams and life.
3.
Tell someone in the society
Accountability is massively underrated. Tell someone you're restarting. Even posting in the group chat once a day builds commitment.
02
The Real Work
Kill the
Procrastination
The biggest obstacle isn't difficulty. It's starting. Every single day.
❌ What doesn't work
"I'll start properly on Monday."
Solving 10 problems in one day, then nothing for a week.
"I need to revise theory before I start solving."
Looking at solutions within 10 minutes of starting.
Comparing your Day 5 to someone else's Month 5.
Waiting to "feel motivated" before opening the laptop.
✅ What actually works
Opening LeetCode right now, even for 20 minutes.
1 problem daily, no exceptions. Even on bad days.
Learning theory through problems — not before them.
Spending real time struggling. That's where learning happens.
Tracking your own streak. Comparing yourself to last week's you.
Using the community — post a question, share a win, ask for help.
🎯
The honest truth about LeetCode
There is no shortcut. No magic video series. No perfect resource. You get better by solving problems — slowly, imperfectly, and repeatedly. The people who succeed aren't smarter. They just sat down and did it, even when they didn't feel like it.

How Problems Work
10 Patterns.
Infinite Problems.
Almost every problem is a variation of one of these. Learn to recognize them and you stop solving from scratch.
01
Two Pointers
Sorted array or need pairs. Move from both ends inward.
02
Sliding Window
Subarray / substring with a constraint. Expand right, shrink left.
03
Fast & Slow Pointers
Cycle detection, middle of linked list. Floyd's algorithm.
04
Binary Search
Not just sorted arrays. Any time you can eliminate half the search space.
05
Tree DFS / BFS
DFS via recursion or stack. BFS via queue for level-order traversal.
06
Graph BFS / DFS
Connected components, shortest path, islands, dependencies.
07
Dynamic Programming
Overlapping subproblems. Memoize recursion. Build a table.
08
Backtracking
Explore all possibilities with pruning. Permutations, combinations, subsets.
09
Heap / Priority Queue
Top-K problems, merge K sorted lists, streaming data.
10
Trie
Prefix trees. Word search, autocomplete, dictionary problems.
💡
How to use these patterns
Don't memorize all 10 at once. Learn each pattern when you encounter it. After Phase 1, start solving problems by topic using the LeetCode Patterns list — group practice builds recognition far faster than random solving.
05
The Society Angle
You're Not
Doing This Alone
Being part of a community is an unfair advantage. Here's how to use it.
Share your attempts, not just solutions
Post "I tried this approach but got stuck here" — not just "I solved it." The struggle is where the learning is, and it normalizes difficulty for everyone.
Ask dumb questions
Every "obvious" question you're afraid to ask is a question 5 other people have. Asking out loud creates the kind of community where everyone gets better faster.
Pair up for accountability
Find one person at your level and tell each other what problem you solved today. That's it. Two-minute check-in. Dramatically improves consistency.
Attend society sessions
Watching someone think through a problem out loud is one of the highest-leverage learning activities. You absorb pattern recognition passively just by being in the room.
Celebrate the small wins
First Easy solved. First Medium solved. First time you recognized a pattern without help. These milestones matter — share them.
No gatekeeping
This community exists to build, not to flex. Whether you're on problem 3 or problem 150 — you belong here and your progress counts.
📢
The culture we're building
The goal isn't a leaderboard. It's a room — physical or digital — where solving problems is the normal thing to do. Where asking for help is expected. Where nobody quits alone. That's the DSA culture we're building here, one problem at a time.
